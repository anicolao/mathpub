# Reader-facing prose style

Write every sentence for one of two readers:

- **the student**, throughout the lesson, examples, exercises, and self-assessment; or
- **the tutor or homeschool parent**, only in clearly labeled teaching tips.

Never address the author, the project team, or the generation process. Never mention prompts,
templates, content production, validation pipelines, projections, review status, or pedagogical
intent inside the publication.

## Sound like Anna's book

Anna’s introductions begin with something the student can picture—a grocery cart, a balance scale,
a traffic intersection, nested boxes—and connect it to the mathematics within a few sentences.
The analogy opens the door; precise definitions and notation then take over.

Use a warm, confident voice:

- speak directly with “you” when giving the student a useful choice or observation;
- use “we” when working through mathematics together;
- prefer familiar words before introducing formal vocabulary;
- keep enthusiasm natural and age-appropriate;
- explain the reason for a rule instead of announcing that it is important.

## Introductions

Open with a concrete situation, pattern, or question that genuinely matches the topic. Then name
the mathematical idea and explain why the student will use it.

Avoid meta-prose:

> This section develops the idea from meaning to procedure, then asks students to explain and
> check their work.

Write to the learner instead:

> A temperature of \(4^\circ\) and a temperature of \(-4^\circ\) are the same distance from zero,
> but they are not the same number. Number sets help us describe exactly what these values have in
> common—and what makes them different.

“In this section, you will…” is acceptable because it addresses the reader. “This section asks
students to…” talks about the reader from outside the book and is not acceptable.

## Explanations and instructions

Describe the mathematical objects actually on the page. Tell the student what to notice, what
choice to make, and why that choice works.

Avoid a generic recipe:

> Record the relevant definition, simplify one operation at a time, and keep equivalent statements
> aligned.

Prefer topic-specific guidance:

> In \(24\div4\times2\), division and multiplication have equal priority. Start at the left:
> \(24\div4=6\), and then multiply \(6\times2=12\). Multiplication does not automatically jump
> ahead of division.

Use short paragraphs around displayed mathematics. A rule should be followed promptly by a
worked instance of that rule.

## Guided examples

Use this reader-visible structure:

- **Problem:** the exact task.
- **Thought Process:** what a capable learner notices before calculating.
- **Steps:** one meaningful transformation per line, with brief reasons where the choice matters.
- **Answer:** a complete result in the requested form.
- **Check:** substitution, estimation, inverse operation, graph, units, or interpretation when
  applicable.

Thought Process is not an author’s commentary. It models the internal questions a student can
reuse: “What is attached to the variable?”, “Which operation is farther left?”, or “Does the square
root simplify before I classify it?”

## Mistakes, summaries, and parent tips

A mistake entry must show believable wrong mathematics beside the correction and explain the
misconception. Name memorable errors when helpful, but do not shame the learner.

A summary should be useful while studying: state the rule, the cue for choosing it, and the check.
Do not merely repeat section headings.

Address parent tips directly and make each one actionable. Good tips specify:

- an object or visual to use;
- what the parent should ask the student to say or mark;
- the misconception the activity reveals; and
- what to try when that misconception appears.

For example, asking a student to draw arrows from an outside factor to every term reveals whether
they understand distribution better than telling a parent to “provide extra practice.”

## Final voice check

Remove or rewrite any sentence that:

- could be pasted unchanged into an unrelated lesson;
- describes students in the third person when it should say “you”;
- explains why the author included something rather than explaining the mathematics;
- sounds like a rubric, lesson plan, generation report, or validation note;
- tells the learner to be careful without naming what to look for;
- gives a command without connecting it to the concrete problem.
