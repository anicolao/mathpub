# Anna-style section standard

## Required shape

1. **Introduction:** a familiar situation, question, or mathematical surprise that leads directly
   to the topic.
2. **What You Will Learn:** three to six observable statements addressed as “you will.”
3. **Teaching:** two or more explanatory subsections with definitions, notation, and concrete
   instructions for the exact mathematics at hand.
4. **Guided Examples:** at least four distinct examples; six to eight for a multi-step topic.
5. **Common Mistakes to Avoid:** specific incorrect work, its cause, and its correction.
6. **Summary:** the decisions, rules, and checks the student should retain.
7. **Teaching Tips for Tutors & Homeschool Parents:** actions, questions, and interventions tied to
   this topic.

## Depth

- Build from meaning to notation to procedure to application.
- Define a term before expecting the student to use it.
- Explain why each important transformation is valid.
- Make examples progress: introductory, routine, misconception-sensitive, multi-step, and applied.
- Give every example a problem, thought process, labeled steps, final answer, and an appropriate
  check.
- Use a table, graph, diagram, verbal model, or physical interpretation when it improves
  understanding.
- Write enough teaching that a student can learn without a lecturer filling in missing reasoning.

## Reject the section when

- headings are followed by generic boilerplate that could fit another topic;
- examples differ only in coefficients or repeat exercise questions;
- important intermediate work is skipped;
- mistakes are generic warnings rather than mathematical examples;
- tutor tips amount to encouragement without a concrete activity or diagnostic question;
- the prose describes the authoring process, generated material, validation machinery, or the
  intended pedagogy instead of teaching the reader.

Write TeX fragments only. Keep answers outside student-facing source fragments.
