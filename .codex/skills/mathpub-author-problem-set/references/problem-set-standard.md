# Problem-set standard

## Student set

Include at least twenty distinct underlying mathematical tasks. A useful distribution is:

- 3 prerequisite or vocabulary checks;
- 7 direct skill questions with varied structures;
- 4 multi-step questions;
- 2 representation or interpretation questions;
- 2 misconception or error-analysis questions;
- 2 applications.

Adjust the distribution to suit the topic, but preserve breadth and progression. Use descriptive
part headings and directions that tell the student what form, work, units, or precision is expected.

Every item must stand on its own. Do not write “solve the preceding problem again,” ask repeatedly
for commentary on one calculation, or count several checks of the same four problems as twenty
questions. A multipart item counts as one numbered question unless its parts are mathematically
independent and separately numbered.

## Answer material

For every numbered item:

- give a concise short answer;
- show intermediate work in the solution;
- explain the decisive choice or operation;
- address signs, branches, restrictions, units, or rejected values;
- finish in the requested form and in context; and
- include an efficient check when useful.

Solutions should teach the student, not report that an answer was computationally verified.

## Variety and quality

Do not make the set a coefficient-swapping worksheet. Mix symbolic, verbal, tabular, graphical,
and contextual representations where relevant. Include realistic distractors and errors based on
the section’s common misconceptions. Avoid values that accidentally remove the skill being tested.

Keep exercises, short answers, and worked solutions in separate TeX fragments.
